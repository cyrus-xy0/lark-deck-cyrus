# Zhongji Innolight AI Lecture Reference Recipe

Source: https://fuqiang.github.io/feishusolution/Zhongji-Innolight-AI-Lecture/index.html

This recipe records what the Cyrus planner and renderer should learn from the
reference deck. Use it for enterprise AI, digital employee, manufacturing
operations, knowledge extraction, and executive lecture-style decks.

## Core Lesson

The reference is not a generic product pitch. It is a business lecture that
turns AI into concrete work scenes:

- Digital employees are framed as new productivity units, not as 1:1 human
  clones.
- The narrative repeatedly moves from management pain to a visible operating
  mechanism: review assistant, meeting recap, proxy review, anomaly radar,
  standard guardian, knowledge management, NPI engineer coaching.
- It uses named work moments and protagonists, especially "NPI engineer Li
  Gong's day", to make invisible expertise memorable.
- It alternates dense proof pages with quote and section pages, so the audience
  has a rhythm: idea, scene, proof, case, advice.

## Required Narrative Pattern

Planner output for similar cases must contain all of these layers:

- **Business scene:** who is working, at what moment, with what input and output.
- **Pain:** what breaks today, why it matters now, and what business consequence
  follows if nothing changes.
- **AI mechanism:** what the agent/digital employee observes, decides, creates,
  or escalates.
- **Human role change:** which human work moves from chasing, checking, or
  remembering into judgment, coaching, or exception handling.
- **Value proof:** metric, case, expected operating improvement, or explicit
  evidence gap. Never hide missing proof behind generic words.
- **Implementation advice:** online data foundation, incentives, compound
  talent, "ask AI first", and small-step iteration.

## Visual Patterns To Reuse

At least 40% of slides in a high-stakes enterprise AI deck should use concrete
web-native visual containers instead of plain cards. For manufacturing / NPI /
quality / supply-chain decks this is a minimum acceptance rule, not a style
preference: at least two body slides must be concrete visual anchors, and no
more than three consecutive body slides may be generic card/table/process
templates.

- Native dashboard or review panel for transparency and quality inspection.
- Phone/chat/meeting recap mock for "AI knows you" scenes.
- Timeline or workday storyboard for role-specific pain.
- Radar or scorecard for coaching and capability gaps.
- Case page with pain/conflict/solution/value arc and a real or source-backed
  scene image.
- Architecture or data-flow page that shows input -> AI processing -> business
  output.
- Quote or section breath page after several dense proof pages.

## Anti-Patterns From The Failed Case

Reject or replan when an outline or rendered deck shows these symptoms:

- Cloud delivery is a Feishu Docx HTML Box when the user asked for a Miaobi /
  Magic cloud HTML page.
- Cover or hero text has not been screenshot-verified and may overlap.
- Slides are a sequence of equal-weight cards, generic 3-up blocks, or repeated
  dashboard-looking panels with no visual center.
- The planner repeats source headings without naming business roles, moments,
  pain, and solution actions.
- Stats pages use ordinal placeholders such as "01 / 02 / 03" when source
  metrics are available.
- Flow/table pages repeat the same message in every step instead of describing
  step-specific inputs, judgments, outputs, or proof needs.

## Acceptance Checklist

Before rendering or publishing:

- Can a sales or solution lead retell the deck as a business story, not as a
  feature list?
- Does every solution page map to a specific operational pain and next action?
- Is there a visible protagonist, operating system, or business artifact in the
  most important section?
- Are source metrics parsed into real numbers/units/labels, or explicitly
  marked as evidence gaps?
- Did visual audit actually run for delivery builds?
- Is the final share link a standalone Magic Page app URL unless the user
  explicitly asked for a document embed?
- Did pitch rehearsal run before publish, and did it avoid a
  `request-more-material` / low-trust outcome for high-stakes decks?
